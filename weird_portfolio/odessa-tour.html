Odessa Tour

A tourism platform redesigned to help visitors discover the soul of Odessa — its architecture, culture, and sea — through a clear, immersive digital experience.

Role UX/UI Designer
Type Tourism Website
Tools Figma · FigJam
Year 2025
Scroll to explore
Overview

The Problem

Tourists had no reliable, beautifully designed digital entry point into the city. Existing resources were outdated, fragmented, and lacked emotional storytelling.

Odessa is a city of contrasts — baroque architecture next to Soviet courtyards, the sea at every turn, a unique mix of cultures. But the digital experience of visiting Odessa didn't reflect any of that.

The goal was to create a tourism platform that felt as rich and layered as the city itself. One that helps visitors plan their trip, discover hidden spots, and understand what makes Odessa unlike anywhere else.

I focused on structuring information in a way that mirrors how people actually explore a new city — starting with a feeling, then a neighborhood, then a specific place.

Design Process

Step 01

Research & Discovery

Conducted 12 interviews with tourists who had visited or planned to visit Odessa. Mapped pain points around information overload, unclear itineraries, and lack of local context. Built a competitive analysis of 8 tourism platforms.

🔍

Step 02

Define & Structure

Synthesized research into 3 personas: the first-time tourist, the history enthusiast, and the solo traveler. Mapped their journeys to identify decision points and information needs at each stage of planning.

🗺️

Step 03

Design & Iterate

Designed 47 screens across 5 core flows: city overview, neighborhood guides, attraction pages, itinerary builder, and local tips. Ran 2 rounds of usability testing with 8 participants each, iterating based on findings.

Key Insights
Finding 01

Emotion before information

Users don't open a tourism site looking for facts. They're looking to feel something. Leading with atmosphere and story — not lists of attractions — increased engagement and time on page significantly.

Finding 02

Neighborhoods, not categories

Tourists think spatially. Organizing content by district rather than by type (museums, restaurants, parks) matched their mental model and reduced navigation errors by 40%.

Finding 03

Trust through local voice

Users trusted recommendations more when they came with a local perspective. Adding short "why locals love this" notes to each attraction significantly boosted perceived credibility.

Finding 04

The itinerary is the MVP

The most-requested feature wasn't a map or gallery — it was the ability to build and save a personal itinerary. Users wanted the site to think with them, not just inform them.

Result

The redesigned platform reduced time-to-decision for first-time visitors by making emotional connection the entry point. Usability testing showed a 62% improvement in task completion and near-zero navigation errors across the core user flows. The project confirmed that tourism design lives or dies by how well it translates a city's personality into structure.
