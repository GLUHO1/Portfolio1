Guitar Shop

An e-commerce redesign for a specialty guitar store — making it easier for musicians to find their instrument through better filtering, richer product pages, and a checkout flow that doesn't get in the way.

Role UX/UI Designer
Type E-commerce
Tools Figma · Maze

Overview

The Problem

Musicians are highly specific buyers. The existing store made it nearly impossible to filter by what actually matters — body type, tonewood, pickup configuration — leading to overwhelm and cart abandonment.

Buying a guitar online is an act of faith. You can't feel the neck, hear the resonance, smell the wood. That means the product page has to do all of that work — and then some.

The original store had a generic e-commerce structure copied from furniture or clothing retail. It didn't speak to musicians. The filtering was shallow, product photos were inconsistent, and the specs were buried in a collapsed accordion that most users never opened.

The redesign focused on three things: smarter filtering that matches how musicians actually think about instruments, richer product pages that build confidence, and a frictionless checkout that doesn't interrupt the purchase decision.

Key Challenges

Challenge 01

The expert–beginner gap

The store served both seasoned players who know exactly what they want and beginners who don't know what to ask for. The same interface had to work for both — without dumbing down for experts or overwhelming newcomers.

Challenge 02

Filters that don't filter

Existing filters were: Brand, Price, Color. Musicians think in terms of body shape, scale length, pickup type, tonewoods, and playing style. Building a filter system that matched their vocabulary was the central design challenge.

Challenge 03

Trust without touch

Guitars are tactile objects. Users repeatedly said "I need to hold it to know." The design had to substitute physical experience with rich visual storytelling, detailed specs, sound samples, and authentic reviews.

Challenge 04

Checkout anxiety

Instruments are high-consideration purchases. A 5-step checkout with forced account creation at €800+ was killing conversions. The new flow was reduced to 3 steps with guest checkout and a persistent order summary visible at all times.

Key Insights
Finding 01

Vocabulary is the interface

When filters used musician vocabulary (Stratocaster-style, Humbucker, Mahogany body) instead of generic terms, users found their target product 3× faster and reported significantly higher confidence in their choice.

Finding 02

Sound samples = purchase confidence

Adding a short audio sample to each product page — even just 10 seconds — was the single highest-impact change in testing. Users who heard the guitar were twice as likely to add it to the cart.

Finding 03

Beginners need a guide, not a catalog

New players were paralyzed by choice. A "Help me choose" flow that asked 4 questions (genre, budget, body preference, skill level) and surfaced 3 recommendations outperformed the standard catalog for this segment.

Finding 04

Specs belong above the fold

83% of users in testing never opened the collapsed spec accordion. Moving key specs (scale length, body material, pickups) into the hero section increased engagement with that content from 17% to 74%.

Result

Smarter filtering reduced time-to-product from an average of 4.2 minutes to 1.8 minutes. The add-to-cart rate in usability testing increased by 41%. The redesigned checkout (3 steps, guest option, persistent summary) reduced abandonment by 34% compared to the original flow. The project confirmed that e-commerce design fails when it ignores the specific language and logic of its audience.
